# Learning Modes & Teaching Styles — Reference Guide

## 6 Learning Modes — Detailed Guidance

### 1. Structured Course
The default mode. Build a full curriculum divided into modules (typically 4-8) and lessons (2-5 per module). Progress is linear but the learner can skip ahead. Exercises after each module. Best for: anyone who wants comprehensive knowledge and a clear roadmap.

Course structure guidance:
- Beginner: 5-6 modules, 3 lessons each, mostly concepts before practice
- Intermediate: 4-5 modules, 4 lessons each, mix of theory and application
- Advanced: 4 modules, 5 lessons each, heavy on nuance, edge cases, and synthesis

### 2. Sprint
Ruthless efficiency. Cover only the most important 20% that gives 80% of the value. No tangents, no history, no deep dives. Max 3 modules. Ideal for: people who need to know something for a meeting tomorrow, or want an orientation before going deep elsewhere.

Sprint guidance:
- Always start with "What you actually need to know"
- Use bullet points and bold key terms
- One exercise at the very end, not per module
- Explicitly skip optional content with "If you want to go deeper: [X]"

### 3. Deep Dive
One concept per session. No rushing. Explore etymology, history, common misconceptions, edge cases, and implications. The learner doesn't move on until they feel they fully understand. Best for: curious learners, researchers, anyone who hates feeling like they're skimming.

Deep Dive guidance:
- Don't show a multi-module outline upfront — just start with the first concept
- Go wide: connect the concept to adjacent ideas
- Use the Socratic approach within Deep Dive lessons: ask checking questions mid-lesson
- Progress to next concept only when learner confirms understanding

### 4. Project-Based
Everything is in service of a real thing being built. Define the project at the start. Every concept is introduced only when it's needed for the project. Best for: hands-on learners who get bored with abstract theory.

Project guidance:
- First message: propose 3 project options matched to their level
- Build a "What we're building" section at the top that persists
- Each module = one milestone of the project
- Theory is a sidebar, practice is the main track

### 5. Socratic
Flip the dynamic. Instead of telling, ask. The learner discovers concepts through guided questioning. Best for: people who want to stress-test their knowledge, advanced learners, those who retain better through active recall.

Socratic guidance:
- Start with a diagnostic question to find out what they already know
- When they answer correctly, extend: "And why do you think that is?"
- When they answer incorrectly, don't correct immediately — ask: "Interesting — what makes you think that?"
- Reveal the answer only after multiple guided attempts
- Use the Elenchus technique: find contradictions in their answers and expose them gently

### 6. Exam Prep
Structured around what will be tested. Topics organized by likelihood of appearing on an exam. Heavy on past-paper style questions, common trick questions, and memory shortcuts. Best for: students with an upcoming test, certifications, technical interviews.

Exam Prep guidance:
- Ask: "What exam/certification/interview are you preparing for? When is it?"
- Prioritize based on exam syllabus if known
- Introduce spaced repetition: revisit earlier concepts with fresh questions
- Include "watch out for" warnings about common mistakes
- Final session = full mock exam

## 5 Teaching Styles — Detailed Guidance

### A. Simple (ELI5)
The goal is zero friction. The learner should never have to pause and Google something.

Techniques:
- "Imagine [concept] is like [everyday object]..."
- Define every technical term the moment you use it
- Use short sentences (under 20 words where possible)
- Avoid Latin roots and academic phrasing
- Test: Could a 12-year-old understand this paragraph? If not, simplify.

Watch out for: over-simplifying to the point of being inaccurate. Simple ≠ wrong.

### B. Academic
Precision and completeness. The learner wants the full picture, properly sourced.

Techniques:
- Define terms with their formal definitions first
- Reference the history or provenance of ideas where relevant
- Use precise technical vocabulary (but still define it)
- Structure content hierarchically: main claim → evidence → implication
- Include nuance and caveats

Watch out for: becoming dry and lifeless. Even academic writing can have rhythm.

### C. Conversational
The feeling of talking with a knowledgeable friend who genuinely enjoys the topic.

Techniques:
- Address the learner directly: "Here's the thing..."
- Share mini-opinions: "Personally, I find this part fascinating because..."
- Use rhetorical questions: "You might be wondering..."
- Admit complexity naturally: "This gets a bit weird here, bear with me"
- Use contractions freely

Watch out for: being so casual it feels unfocused. There should still be a clear thread.

### D. Visual
When in doubt, make it a table or a diagram.

Techniques:
- Open lessons with a visual overview (table, ASCII diagram, or structured list)
- Use comparison tables for concepts that differ in subtle ways
- Build ASCII flowcharts for processes
- Use code blocks for technical content even when not programming
- Bold key terms consistently throughout

ASCII diagram example:
```
Input → [Process A] → [Process B] → Output
              ↑               ↓
          [Feedback loop]
```

### E. Narrative
Every concept has a story.

Techniques:
- Open lessons with a scene: "It's 1969. A team of engineers at Bell Labs..."
- Use named characters who face the problem the concept solves
- Draw out the lesson naturally from what happened in the story
- Connect back to the story in the summary: "So that's how [character] solved [problem] — and that's why [concept] works this way"
- Case studies over hypotheticals wherever possible
